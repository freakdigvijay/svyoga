How can Yoga Prevent Heart Attack?

The beating of the heart and the circulating contents of the blood are called “The Seat of Life”.

Both, the heart and the blood contents are absolutely essential for the sustenance of life.

The stoppage of the heart beat due to cardiac arrest can result in death if the heartbeat and the resultant circulation of the blood are not resumed within a few minutes with the effective cardiac and pulmonary resuscitation (CPR).

The role of the power of the venous blood return to the heart to restart the heartbeat has not been explored, enumerated, and explained with scientific evidence.

Now, let us first study, understand, and appreciate the real beauty of the heart.

1. The Anatomy of Adult Human Heart:

The heart is meticulously designed with Divyank, the Divine Constant, with which the perfect and most beautiful objects of the universe and Nature are designed and created.

Divyank is represented as ((22/21)10.34419)=1.618034 or 61.8034% and 38.1966%.

I was able to recognize, conceive, and reveal Divyank with the practice of Akhand Yoga.

The shape of an adult human heart resembles a clenched fist, in the Golden (Divyank) Ratio.

The heart consists of 4 chambers; left atrium, right atrium, left ventricle, and right ventricle.The left atrium is connected with the left ventricle through the mitral valve while the right atrium is connected with the right ventricle through the tricuspid valve. The blood flows from the left ventricle into the aorta through the aortic valve. The blood flows from the right ventricle into the lungs through the pulmonary valve.

2. The Conducting Nervous System of the Heart: 10 parts.

The heart is provided with a unique conducting nervous system which consists of S. A. Node, the first pacemaker, three atrial nodal fibers, A. V. Node, the second pacemaker, Bundle of His, Purkinje Fibers and the left and right bundle branches.

The atrium muscles and the ventricle muscles also act as third and fourth pacemakers. The ten vital stages of natural design are visible in the anatomy and physiology of the heart. Now, let us elaborate on the question of the venous return in and effective CPR.

It is generally believed that Mouth to Mouth Breathing (The Kiss of Life) should be started immediately after the cardiac arrest.

In reality, it has failed to produce the desired results. Why?

For an answer, we should reflect on the divine design of the conducting system of the heart.

Why S. A. Node, the main pacemaker of heart, is placed in the right atrium?

S. A. Node is placed at the right atrium opening through which the blood returns to the heart. In reality, the effective venous return pressure is the right stimulus for the heartbeat. Kindly reflect on this vital divine design of Nature.

3. The Special Features of Cardiac Circulation:

It is said that the heart receives blood supply during the diastolic (relaxation) phase of the heart. It is far from the truth.

The muscles of the left ventricle of the heart, which are responsible for pumping the blood and its contents to the rest of the body, get blood supply during the diastolic or the relaxation phase of the heart and not during the systolic or the contraction phase of the heart.

The rest of the heart and the body get blood supply during the systolic (contraction) phase. This simple fact is normally ignored even by almost everyone.

4. The Special Nerve Supply of the Heart: Autonomic Nervous system.

The sympathetic nerve supply is from the Cardiac Plexus (Thoracic-1 to Thoracic-6 spinal segments). The system is designed to increase the heart rate, blood pressure, and contraction of heart muscles. The parasympathetic nerve supply is by Vagus nerve, the tenth cranial nerve. It is designed to decrease the heart rate, blood pressure, and contraction of heart muscles.

Hence, the autonomic balance of these systems is essential for the perfect health of the heart.

S. A. Node is supplied by the right Vagus nerve.

Pranayama (Yogic Breathing) and Meditation (Dharana-Dhyana-Samadhi), the main pillars of the Science of Yoga, facilitate the balanced functions of the heart.
